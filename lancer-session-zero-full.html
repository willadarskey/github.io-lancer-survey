The Setting

It is the year 5016u. Humanity has been spreading across the stars for thousands of years, and it shows — in everything.

If you were born near Cradle, you have never checked a price before buying food. You have never watched someone die of something curable. Your body has been maintained since birth by medical technology so routine it has no name, only a scheduled appointment. The omninet streams culture, news, and connection into every surface you own. You have opinions about which blink gate terminal has the best departure lounges. When you think about Union, if you think about it at all, it feels something like gravity — invisible, constant, and not worth worrying about. You have never needed to worry about it. That is, itself, the point.

If you were born on the frontier, you know what things cost because you have gone without them. You have watched infections become fevers become graves. You have eaten the same protein culture for six months because the supply barge was late, and when it finally arrived the prices had gone up. You know your neighbors not because you chose them but because survival made the choice for you. The omninet reaches you — technically — but the blink gate is two months away at sub-light, and the gate fees are set by whoever showed up and decided they owned the terminal. Union is coming. It has been coming your whole life. You have started to wonder what it will want when it gets here.

Union holds the galaxy together through three levers: the blink gates, the omninet, and manna, the universal currency. Control all three and you control everything. Union does. It prefers to govern from a distance — letting worlds sort themselves out, stepping in only when things become too broken to ignore. When it steps in, it arrives with warships. People on the frontier have learned that liberation and death can travel in the same fleet. The difference between them is a decision made very far away, by people who will never see the consequences.

Four corpo-states fill the space Union leaves behind, selling mechs, weapons, and technology to anyone with manna to spend. They operate under Union's regulations the way water operates under gravity: technically in compliance, always looking for lower ground.

General Massive Systems is everywhere — not because anyone chose them, but because they were already there. GMS makes the Everest, the frame every pilot drops in on their first mission. Their gear is openly licensed, priced for access, and repairable by anyone with a wrench. You'll find GMS components in the most advanced SSC build and the most desperate frontier salvage job. They are the shared infrastructure of the galaxy's military economy — the argument, made in steel and composite, that common ownership works.

IPS-Northstar builds for people who work for a living. Heavy, durable, deliberately unglamorous — designed to take damage and bring the pilot home. The culture is maritime and industrial. Their mechs have dents that never quite got hammered out. There is something honest about them, which makes it easier to forget that honest and good are not the same thing.

Smith-Shimano Corpro makes the most beautiful machines in the galaxy, and they know it. SSC frames are agile, elegant, and personal — each one a statement about who the pilot is. The aesthetics are solarpunk. The business model is not. Identity is their product. Access is conditional on what you can pay.

Harrison Armory doesn't bother with aesthetics. They make the most powerful weapons available outside Union's core military and sell them with a philosophy attached: security is purchased, strength is its own justification, and the market decides who deserves either. They are completely open about what they are. That openness is, somehow, the most unsettling thing about them.

And then there is HORUS — which is not a corpo-state, not a manufacturer in any sense the other four would recognize, and possibly not an organization at all. Their technology appears on the omninet without explanation: mech modifications, weapon systems, things that shouldn't be possible given anything anyone understands about physics. It spreads freely. Nobody knows who builds it. Nobody agrees on what HORUS wants, or whether wanting is even the right framework for whatever HORUS is. What is clear is this: they are hostile to hierarchy, hostile to enclosure, and hostile to the idea that any person or organization should hold permanent control over any technology. The four corpo-states are all, in their different ways, answering the question of who gets to own things. HORUS is the only faction in the galaxy still asking why that's the question.

You are a lancer. A mechanized cavalry pilot of particular note, operating somewhere between those two worlds — close enough to the frontier that mechs are necessary, far enough from Cradle that nobody is coming to save you. Mechs are deployed when all other options have failed. When the drop klaxons sound, it is up to you. Whatever your history, you are human: exactly as flawed and capable as that has always meant.
